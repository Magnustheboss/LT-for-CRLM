Clinical Case Studies

The following case studies illustrate how prognostic scoring systems and patient selection criteria are applied in real-world clinical scenarios. Each case demonstrates the decision-making process and expected outcomes for different patient profiles.

Case 1: Ideal Candidate for LT

EXCELLENT CANDIDATE

Patient Presentation

Patient: 52-year-old female with synchronous liver-only colorectal metastases

Parameter Value Significance
Primary Tumor Right colon, T3N0M1 Node-negative primary (favorable)
Metastases 3 liver lesions, largest 4.2 cm Multiple lesions but small size
CEA Level 32 ng/mL Mildly elevated (< 80)
Performance Status ECOG 0 Fully active
Comorbidities None significant No contraindications

Prognostic Assessment

Scoring System Score Risk Category Expected 5-Year OS
Oslo Score 0 Favorable 80-92%
FCRS 1 Favorable 75-80%
PET-MTV 45 cm³ Favorable (< 70) 63.6%

Molecular Profile

  • BRAF: Wild-type (✓ eligible)
  • KRAS: Wild-type (favorable)
  • TP53: Wild-type (favorable)
  • MSI: Microsatellite stable (✓ eligible)
  • RAS/TP53 co-mutations: Absent (favorable)

Clinical Course

Bridging Therapy: 6 months of FOLFOX + bevacizumab

  • Excellent response: 80% reduction in tumor burden
  • CEA normalized to 8 ng/mL
  • No disease progression
  • Excellent tolerance to chemotherapy

Treatment Decision

Recommendation: PROCEED WITH LIVER TRANSPLANTATION

Rationale: All favorable prognostic factors; excellent response to bridging chemotherapy; no exclusion criteria; expected 5-year OS 80-90%.

Outcome

Surgical Course: Deceased donor liver transplantation performed without complications

  • Operative time: 4.5 hours
  • Blood loss: 800 mL
  • Graft function: Excellent immediate function
  • ICU stay: 2 days
  • Hospital stay: 10 days

Follow-up (36 months post-LT):

  • No recurrence detected
  • Excellent graft function (INR 1.1, bilirubin 0.8, albumin 4.2)
  • Stable immunosuppression (tacrolimus level 8-10 ng/mL)
  • No episodes of acute rejection
  • Excellent quality of life

Key Learning Point: Ideal candidates with favorable prognostic factors achieve excellent long-term outcomes comparable to traditional LT indications (HCC within Milan criteria).

Case 2: Borderline Candidate

ACCEPTABLE CANDIDATE

Patient Presentation

Patient: 58-year-old male with metachronous liver-only colorectal metastases

Parameter Value Significance
Primary Tumor Sigmoid colon, T3N2M0 (3 years prior) Node-positive primary
Disease-Free Interval 36 months Long interval (favorable)
Metastases 6 liver lesions, largest 6.0 cm Multiple lesions, borderline size
CEA Level 85 ng/mL Elevated (> 80)
Performance Status ECOG 1 Restricted to light activity

Prognostic Assessment

Scoring System Score Risk Category Expected 5-Year OS
Oslo Score 2 Intermediate ~60%
FCRS 2 Favorable 75-80%
PET-MTV 75 cm³ Intermediate (≥ 70) 25%

Molecular Profile

  • BRAF: Wild-type (✓ eligible)
  • KRAS: G12V mutation (unfavorable)
  • TP53: Wild-type (favorable)
  • MSI: Microsatellite stable (✓ eligible)
  • RAS/TP53 co-mutations: Absent (favorable)

Clinical Course

Bridging Therapy: 9 months of FOLFOXIRI + cetuximab (anti-EGFR for KRAS wild-type would be preferred, but patient has KRAS mutation)

  • Partial response: 40% reduction in tumor burden
  • CEA decreased to 45 ng/mL (still elevated)
  • Stable disease after 6 months
  • Grade 2 neuropathy from oxaliplatin

Multidisciplinary Discussion

Key Questions:

  • Is intermediate Oslo Score (2) acceptable given other factors?
  • Does high PET-MTV (75 cm³) outweigh favorable FCRS?
  • Should extended bridging therapy be considered?
  • What is patient's understanding of outcomes and risks?

Team Consensus: Acceptable candidate for LT with careful patient counseling about intermediate prognosis. Extended bridging therapy recommended to optimize disease control.

Treatment Decision

Recommendation: PROCEED WITH LIVER TRANSPLANTATION AFTER EXTENDED BRIDGING

Rationale: Intermediate risk but no absolute contraindications. Extended bridging therapy (additional 3-6 months) recommended to improve metabolic tumor volume and CEA level. Expected 5-year OS 50-70%.

Outcome

Extended Bridging Therapy (3 additional months):

  • Additional 20% reduction in tumor burden
  • CEA decreased to 28 ng/mL
  • PET-MTV improved to 62 cm³ (now favorable)
  • Completion of bridging therapy after 12 months total

Surgical Course: Living donor liver transplantation (sister donor)

  • Graft type: Right lobe LDLT
  • Operative time: 6 hours
  • Graft function: Good initial function
  • Hospital stay: 14 days

Follow-up (24 months post-LT):

  • 12 months: Pulmonary metastasis detected (1.2 cm)
  • Treatment: Thoracoscopic resection performed
  • 18 months: Peritoneal nodule detected
  • Treatment: Systemic chemotherapy restarted (FOLFOX)
  • 24 months: Stable disease on chemotherapy; good graft function

Key Learning Point: Borderline candidates benefit from extended bridging therapy to optimize disease control. Even with recurrence, median OS after relapse is 37.1 months, providing opportunity for salvage therapy.

Case 3: Poor Candidate (Not Recommended for LT)

POOR CANDIDATE

Patient Presentation

Patient: 62-year-old male with extensive liver-only colorectal metastases

Parameter Value Significance
Primary Tumor Rectum, T4N2M1 (18 months prior) Node-positive, advanced primary
Disease-Free Interval 18 months Short interval
Metastases 12+ liver lesions, largest 8.5 cm Extensive disease burden
CEA Level 245 ng/mL Markedly elevated
Chemotherapy Response Progressive disease Aggressive biology

Prognostic Assessment

Scoring System Score Risk Category Expected 5-Year OS
Oslo Score 4 Unfavorable 8.3%
FCRS 5 Unfavorable < 10%
PET-MTV 180 cm³ Unfavorable (>> 70) < 10%

Molecular Profile

  • BRAF: V600E mutation (✗ ABSOLUTE EXCLUSION)
  • KRAS: G12C mutation
  • TP53: R248Q mutation
  • MSI: Microsatellite instability (✗ EXCLUSION)
  • RAS/TP53 co-mutations: Present (very unfavorable)

Clinical Assessment

Multiple Exclusion Criteria Present:

  • ✗ BRAF V600E mutation (absolute exclusion)
  • ✗ Microsatellite instability (exclusion)
  • ✗ RAS/TP53 co-mutations (very poor prognosis)
  • ✗ Progressive disease on chemotherapy (aggressive biology)
  • ✗ Markedly elevated CEA (extensive disease)
  • ✗ Oslo Score 4 (worst prognostic category)

Treatment Decision

Recommendation: NOT A CANDIDATE FOR LIVER TRANSPLANTATION

Rationale: Multiple absolute and relative exclusion criteria. BRAF V600E mutation and microsatellite instability are absolute contraindications. RAS/TP53 co-mutations associated with median OS of only 21 months. Expected 5-year OS with LT would be < 10%. Patient would be better served by palliative care.

Alternative Management

Recommended Approach: Palliative care with systemic chemotherapy

  • Chemotherapy: Continue FOLFOX or consider alternative regimen given progression
  • Targeted Therapy: Consider sotorasib (KRAS G12C inhibitor) given mutation status
  • Locoregional Therapy: TACE or SIRT for symptom control if needed
  • Supportive Care: Palliative care team involvement; symptom management; quality of life focus
  • Clinical Trials: Enrollment in trials for advanced CRLM if eligible

Follow-up (12 months):

  • Continued progression despite multiple chemotherapy regimens
  • Development of hepatic encephalopathy
  • Palliative care team managing symptoms
  • Patient and family focused on quality of life

Key Learning Point: Not all patients with CRLM are candidates for LT. Careful patient selection using prognostic scoring systems and molecular profiling is essential to identify patients who will benefit from transplantation and avoid offering futile treatment to those with poor prognosis.

Case 4: Excluded Candidate (Extrahepatic Disease)

EXCLUDED

Patient Presentation

Patient: 55-year-old female with colorectal metastases to liver and lungs

Key Finding

Extrahepatic Metastases: Multiple bilateral pulmonary nodules (largest 2.5 cm)

Exclusion Criterion: Extrahepatic metastases are an absolute contraindication for LT (except prior resected pulmonary metastases)

Liver Disease Assessment

  • Liver Metastases: 4 lesions, largest 3.5 cm (would be resectable)
  • Oslo Score: 1 (favorable)
  • FCRS: 0 (favorable)
  • PET-MTV: 35 cm³ (favorable)

Treatment Decision

Recommendation: NOT A CANDIDATE FOR LIVER TRANSPLANTATION

Rationale: Although liver disease is favorable, extrahepatic pulmonary metastases are an absolute exclusion criterion for LT. This patient should be considered for liver resection combined with pulmonary resection or locoregional therapy for lung lesions.

Alternative Management

  • Liver Resection: Hepatic resection of 4 metastases
  • Pulmonary Management: Thoracoscopic resection of lung nodules
  • Systemic Therapy: Adjuvant chemotherapy post-resection

Key Learning Point: Thorough staging with PET-CT is essential to identify extrahepatic disease. Even patients with excellent liver disease prognosis are not candidates for LT if extrahepatic metastases are present.

Case 5: Transformation from Marginal to Excellent Candidate

EXCELLENT RESPONSE TO BRIDGING

Initial Presentation

Patient: 54-year-old male with newly diagnosed unresectable CRLM

Parameter Initial After 12 Months Bridging
Largest Tumor 7.2 cm 3.8 cm (47% reduction)
CEA Level 125 ng/mL 18 ng/mL (86% reduction)
PET-MTV 95 cm³ 42 cm³ (56% reduction)
Oslo Score 3 (unfavorable) 1 (favorable)
FCRS 3 (unfavorable) 1 (favorable)

Bridging Therapy

  • Regimen: FOLFOXIRI + bevacizumab
  • Duration: 12 months (6 cycles)
  • Response: Partial response with excellent tolerance
  • Molecular Profile: BRAF wild-type, KRAS wild-type, TP53 wild-type, MSI negative

Transformation of Risk Profile

Initial Assessment: Marginal/poor candidate based on unfavorable scoring systems and high disease burden

After Bridging Therapy: Excellent candidate based on dramatic response and transformation to favorable prognostic profile

Key Insight: Response to bridging chemotherapy is a dynamic prognostic factor. Patients with excellent response may be "upgraded" from poor to excellent candidates.

Treatment Decision

Recommendation: PROCEED WITH LIVER TRANSPLANTATION

Rationale: Excellent response to bridging therapy has transformed patient from marginal to excellent candidate. Current prognostic scores are favorable. Expected 5-year OS 75-85%.

Outcome

Surgical Course: Deceased donor liver transplantation

  • Operative time: 4.8 hours
  • Graft function: Excellent
  • Hospital stay: 11 days

Follow-up (36 months post-LT):

  • No recurrence detected
  • Excellent graft function
  • Excellent quality of life

Key Learning Point: Extended bridging chemotherapy can dramatically improve prognostic scores and disease burden. Patients with excellent response to bridging therapy may achieve excellent long-term outcomes despite initially unfavorable presentation.

Summary of Case Studies

These case studies illustrate the importance of:

  • Comprehensive prognostic assessment using multiple scoring systems
  • Molecular profiling to identify exclusion criteria
  • Careful patient selection to optimize outcomes
  • Extended bridging therapy for marginal candidates
  • Multidisciplinary team discussion for complex cases
  • Patient counseling about realistic expectations
