Glossary of Terms

A comprehensive reference for medical, surgical, and transplant-related terminology used throughout this resource.

Clinical and Oncological Terms

CEA (Carcinoembryonic Antigen)

A tumor marker protein produced by colorectal cancer cells. Elevated levels (typically > 5 ng/mL) suggest higher disease burden. Used in the Oslo Score (> 80 µg/L) and FCRS (> 200 ng/mL) for prognostic assessment.

CRLM (Colorectal Liver Metastases)

Secondary liver tumors that originate from colorectal cancer. Approximately 50% of colorectal cancer patients develop CRLM during their disease course. Can be synchronous (present at time of primary diagnosis) or metachronous (develop later).

DFI (Disease-Free Interval)

The time between resection of the primary colorectal tumor and detection of the first metastasis. A short DFI (< 12 months) indicates aggressive disease and is a risk factor in the FCRS.

DFS (Disease-Free Survival)

The length of time after treatment during which a patient survives without any sign of cancer recurrence. In CRLM patients after LT, median DFS is typically 11.8 months despite excellent overall survival.

ECOG (Eastern Cooperative Oncology Group) Performance Status

A scale (0-4) measuring patient functional status. ECOG 0 = fully active; ECOG 1 = restricted strenuous activity; ECOG 2+ = mostly bedbound. Patients with ECOG > 1 are generally not candidates for LT.

HTL (High Tumor Load)

Extensive liver metastases that would preclude standard liver resection. LT offers a curative option for HTL CRLM patients who would otherwise have no surgical option.

Metastasis / Metastatic Disease

Cancer that has spread from the primary site to distant organs. In CRLM, the liver is the site of metastatic disease originating from the colon or rectum.

OS (Overall Survival)

The length of time from diagnosis or treatment start until death from any cause. The primary endpoint in most CRLM trials. LT + chemotherapy achieves 56.6% 5-year OS compared to 12.6% with chemotherapy alone.

Progressive Disease

Cancer that continues to grow despite treatment. In CRLM, progressive disease on chemotherapy is a risk factor (included in Oslo Score) indicating aggressive tumor biology.

Resectable vs. Unresectable

Resectable: Liver metastases that can be surgically removed while preserving adequate liver function. Unresectable: Metastases that cannot be removed due to extent, location, or patient factors. LT is indicated for permanently unresectable CRLM.

Synchronous vs. Metachronous

Synchronous CRLM: Liver metastases present at the time of primary colorectal cancer diagnosis. Metachronous CRLM: Liver metastases that develop after resection of the primary tumor.

Molecular and Genetic Terms

BRAF Mutation

A mutation in the BRAF gene that activates the MAPK signaling pathway. BRAF V600E mutations are associated with poor prognosis and are an ABSOLUTE EXCLUSION criterion for LT in CRLM per IHPBA guidelines.

KRAS Mutation

A mutation in the KRAS gene affecting growth signaling. Present in approximately 40% of colorectal cancers. KRAS mutations are associated with reduced response to anti-EGFR therapy but are not an exclusion criterion for LT.

Microsatellite Instability (MSI)

A condition where DNA repair mechanisms are defective, leading to accumulation of mutations. MSI-high tumors are associated with poor prognosis in CRLM and are an EXCLUSION criterion for LT.

Mismatch Repair (MMR) Deficiency

Loss of function of DNA mismatch repair proteins (MLH1, MSH2, MSH6, PMS2). Causes microsatellite instability. Patients must be MMR proficient to be candidates for LT in CRLM.

Molecular Profiling

Genetic testing of tumors to identify mutations and other molecular characteristics. Essential for CRLM patient selection, including assessment of BRAF, KRAS, NRAS, TP53, and MSI status.

NRAS Mutation

A mutation in the NRAS gene affecting growth signaling. Similar to KRAS mutations in prognostic significance. Not an exclusion criterion for LT but indicates need for careful patient selection.

RAS/TP53 Co-mutations

Concurrent mutations in both RAS genes (KRAS or NRAS) and TP53. Associated with significantly worse outcomes (median OS 21 months vs. 60 months without co-mutations). Patients should be carefully counseled about poor prognosis.

TP53 Mutation

A mutation in the TP53 tumor suppressor gene. Present in approximately 50% of colorectal cancers. TP53 mutations alone are not an exclusion criterion, but RAS/TP53 co-mutations are associated with poor outcomes.

Wild-Type

The normal, non-mutated form of a gene. For example, "BRAF wild-type" means the patient does not have a BRAF V600E mutation and is eligible for LT consideration.

Imaging and Radiological Terms

18F-FDG PET/CT

Positron emission tomography/computed tomography using fluorodeoxyglucose tracer. Shows both anatomical (CT) and metabolic (PET) information. Essential for CRLM evaluation to assess disease burden and detect extrahepatic metastases.

CT (Computed Tomography)

Cross-sectional imaging using X-rays to create detailed images of internal organs. Standard imaging for assessing liver metastases, measuring tumor size, and evaluating resectability.

FDG (Fluorodeoxyglucose)

A glucose analog labeled with radioactive fluorine-18. Used in PET imaging to detect metabolically active tumors. Higher uptake indicates more aggressive disease.

MRI (Magnetic Resonance Imaging)

Imaging using magnetic fields and radio waves to create detailed images. Particularly useful for characterizing liver lesions and detecting small metastases.

PET-MTV (Metabolic Tumor Volume)

The total volume of metabolically active tumor tissue measured on 18F-FDG PET/CT. Measured in cubic centimeters (cm³). PET-MTV < 70 cm³ is associated with favorable outcomes; ≥ 70 cm³ indicates high disease burden.

Segmentation

In imaging, the process of delineating tumor boundaries to calculate volume. Can be automatic (computer-assisted) or semi-automatic (with manual adjustment).

SUV (Standardized Uptake Value)

A measure of FDG uptake intensity on PET imaging. Higher SUV indicates more metabolically active disease. Used to assess tumor aggressiveness.

Transplant-Related Terms

Allograft / Allogeneic Transplant

Transplantation of tissue or organ from one individual to another (as opposed to autograft, which is from the same individual). All liver transplants are allografts.

Bridge Therapy / Bridging Chemotherapy

Systemic chemotherapy given to patients while awaiting liver transplantation. Maintains disease control during the transplant evaluation and waitlist period. Minimum 3 months recommended; often 6-12 months.

DDLT (Deceased Donor Liver Transplantation)

Liver transplantation using an organ from a deceased donor. Requires waitlist time; organ quality varies. Standard approach in most transplant centers.

Graft

The transplanted organ. In LT, the graft is the donor liver that replaces the recipient's native liver.

Graft Rejection

Immune attack on the transplanted organ by the recipient's immune system. Acute rejection occurs within weeks to months; chronic rejection develops over years. Managed with immunosuppression.

Hepatectomy

Surgical removal of liver tissue. Total hepatectomy: Removal of the entire native liver (performed during LT). Partial hepatectomy: Removal of part of the liver (performed during resection).

Immunosuppression / Immunosuppressive Therapy

Medications that suppress the immune system to prevent graft rejection. Common agents include tacrolimus, mycophenolate, corticosteroids, and everolimus. Must be taken for life after transplantation.

LDLT (Living Donor Liver Transplantation)

Liver transplantation using a portion of liver from a living donor (usually a family member or altruistic donor). Offers advantages of shorter cold ischemia time and better graft quality. Associated with 71.4% 3-year OS in CRLM patients.

MELD Score (Model for End-Stage Liver Disease)

A scoring system predicting 3-month mortality in patients with liver disease. Used for deceased donor organ allocation. CRLM patients may have lower MELD scores and longer waitlist times than patients with cirrhosis.

Native Liver

The recipient's original liver that is removed during transplantation. In CRLM patients, the native liver is removed to eliminate all metastatic disease.

Perioperative

The period surrounding surgery, including pre-operative (before), intra-operative (during), and post-operative (after) phases.

RAPID (Rapid Access Percutaneous Intervention Drainage)

An innovative surgical technique combining portal vein embolization and percutaneous drainage to enable rapid liver resection or transplantation in patients with extensive disease.

Recipient

The patient receiving the transplanted organ. In LT for CRLM, the recipient is the colorectal cancer patient with unresectable liver metastases.

Retransplantation

A second or subsequent liver transplantation. May be needed if the first graft fails due to rejection, recurrence, or other complications.

Waitlist Time

The period between listing for transplantation and actual transplant surgery. Varies geographically; typically 6-18 months in Europe, highly variable in the United States.

Treatment-Related Terms

Anti-EGFR Therapy

Targeted therapy against epidermal growth factor receptor. Examples include cetuximab and panitumumab. Effective in KRAS wild-type colorectal cancers. Used in bridging chemotherapy regimens.

Anti-VEGF Therapy

Targeted therapy against vascular endothelial growth factor. Examples include bevacizumab and aflibercept. Inhibits angiogenesis (blood vessel formation). Used in bridging chemotherapy regimens.

Chemotherapy

Systemic cancer treatment using cytotoxic drugs. In CRLM, common regimens include FOLFOX, FOLFIRI, and FOLFOXIRI, often combined with targeted therapy.

FOLFIRI

Chemotherapy regimen combining fluorouracil, leucovorin, and irinotecan. Used for colorectal cancer treatment and CRLM bridging therapy.

FOLFOX

Chemotherapy regimen combining fluorouracil, leucovorin, and oxaliplatin. Commonly used for colorectal cancer treatment and CRLM bridging therapy.

FOLFOXIRI

Triplet chemotherapy regimen combining fluorouracil, leucovorin, irinotecan, and oxaliplatin. More intensive than doublet regimens; used for advanced CRLM.

Locoregional Therapy

Treatment directed at tumors in a specific region (the liver). Examples include TACE, SIRT, RFA, and hepatic artery infusion. May be used as bridge therapy before LT.

Palliative Care

Medical care focused on symptom relief and quality of life rather than cure. For CRLM patients not candidates for LT or resection, palliative chemotherapy is the standard approach.

RFA (Radiofrequency Ablation)

Minimally invasive technique using heat to destroy tumors. Can be used as bridge therapy for small CRLM lesions before LT.

SIRT (Selective Internal Radiation Therapy)

Locoregional therapy using radioactive microspheres injected into hepatic artery to deliver radiation to liver tumors. Can be used as bridge therapy before LT.

TACE (Transarterial Chemoembolization)

Locoregional therapy combining chemotherapy and embolization of hepatic artery. Starves tumors of blood supply while delivering chemotherapy. Can be used as bridge therapy before LT.

Scoring System Terms

FCRS (Fong Clinical Risk Score)

A prognostic scoring system originally developed for liver resection in CRLM, now validated for LT. Includes 5 components: node-positive primary, DFI < 12 months, > 1 tumor, CEA > 200, and largest tumor > 5 cm. Score 0-2 is favorable; ≥ 3 is unfavorable.

IHPBA (International Hepato-Pancreato-Biliary Association)

International professional organization that published consensus guidelines on LT for CRLM in 2021. Guidelines provide evidence-based recommendations for patient selection, graft selection, and outcomes assessment.

Oslo Score

A prognostic scoring system specifically developed for LT in CRLM. Includes 4 components: tumor size ≥ 5.5 cm, progressive disease on chemo, CEA > 80, and time to LT < 2 years. Score 0-2 is favorable; 3-4 is unfavorable. Excellent prognostic discrimination (10-year OS 88.9% for score 0-1).

Prognostic Score

A numerical system combining multiple factors to predict patient outcomes. Used to stratify patients into risk categories and guide treatment decisions.

Risk Stratification

The process of categorizing patients into risk groups (favorable, intermediate, unfavorable) based on prognostic factors. Guides patient selection and counseling.

Miscellaneous Terms

Comorbidity

The presence of one or more additional diseases or conditions in a patient. Significant comorbidities may preclude patients from LT consideration.

Contraindication

A condition or factor that makes a particular treatment inadvisable or unsafe. Absolute contraindications preclude treatment entirely; relative contraindications require careful consideration.

Curative Intent

Treatment aimed at curing the disease rather than providing palliation. LT for CRLM is a curative approach, unlike palliative chemotherapy.

Efficacy

The ability of a treatment to produce the desired effect. LT for CRLM has demonstrated efficacy in improving overall survival compared to chemotherapy alone.

Hazard Ratio (HR)

A statistical measure comparing the risk of an event (e.g., death) between two groups. HR < 1 indicates reduced risk. In TransMet, LT + chemo had HR 0.37 vs. chemo alone (63% risk reduction).

Intention-to-Treat (ITT)

A trial analysis approach including all randomized patients regardless of whether they completed the treatment. Provides real-world outcomes; TransMet ITT analysis showed 56.6% 5-year OS.

Multidisciplinary Team (MDT)

A team of specialists from different disciplines (surgery, oncology, transplant, radiology) working together to evaluate and treat patients. Essential for CRLM patient selection and management.

Per-Protocol Analysis

Trial analysis including only patients who completed the assigned treatment as planned. More optimistic than ITT analysis; TransMet per-protocol showed 73.3% 5-year OS.

Randomized Controlled Trial (RCT)

A study design where participants are randomly assigned to treatment groups. Provides the highest level of evidence. TransMet is the first RCT comparing LT + chemo to chemo alone for CRLM.

Recurrence

Return of cancer after treatment. In CRLM after LT, recurrence occurs in 78.3% of patients but with indolent behavior (median OS after relapse 37.1 months).

Survival Curve

A graph showing the percentage of patients surviving over time. Kaplan-Meier curves are commonly used to display survival data in clinical trials.
