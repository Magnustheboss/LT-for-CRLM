Frequently Asked Questions

Find answers to common questions about liver transplantation for colorectal liver metastases.

Refer patients with permanently unresectable, liver-only colorectal metastases who meet the following criteria:

  • Good performance status (ECOG 0-1)
  • Adequate response to systemic chemotherapy (≥3 months of therapy)
  • No extrahepatic metastases (except prior resected pulmonary metastases)
  • BRAF wild-type (V600E mutation is exclusion)
  • Microsatellite stable and mismatch repair proficient
  • No significant comorbidities precluding surgery

Early referral is recommended when disease is diagnosed as unresectable, as bridging chemotherapy can be optimized while transplant evaluation proceeds.

Both are prognostic scoring systems, but they differ in origin and application:

Aspect Oslo Score FCRS
Developed for LT in CRLM (2006) Liver resection (1999); adapted for LT
Components 4 factors 5 factors
Prognostic power Excellent (7.6-11.0× discrimination) Good (1.9-2.0× discrimination)
Best use Primary risk stratification Secondary confirmation

Recommendation: Use Oslo Score as primary tool; FCRS for confirmation in borderline cases.

PET-MTV (metabolic tumor volume) is increasingly important and should be part of standard pre-LT evaluation:

  • Prognostic Value: PET-MTV < 70 cm³ shows 2.5× better 5-year OS (63.6% vs. 25%)
  • Biological Information: Captures tumor aggressiveness beyond size alone
  • Dual Purpose: Assesses disease burden AND excludes extrahepatic metastases
  • Complementary: Provides information not captured by Oslo Score or FCRS

Recommendation: Obtain 18F-FDG PET/CT as part of standard pre-LT workup. MTV < 70 cm³ is favorable; ≥ 70 cm³ warrants careful consideration.

RAS/TP53 co-mutations are a negative prognostic factor but not an absolute exclusion:

  • Median OS: 21 months (vs. 60 months without co-mutations)
  • Hazard Ratio: 4.0 (95% CI 1.7-9.3)
  • Recommendation: Patients with RAS/TP53 co-mutations should be carefully counseled about poor prognosis
  • Alternative Consideration: May be better served by alternative treatment strategies

In contrast, BRAF V600E mutations ARE an absolute exclusion criterion and preclude transplantation.

Yes, LDLT is an excellent option for CRLM and may offer superior outcomes:

  • 3-Year OS: 71.4% (LDLT) vs. 51.4% (DDLT)
  • Advantages:
    • Shorter cold ischemia time
    • Better graft quality
    • Shorter waitlist time
    • Planned surgery with optimal timing
  • Donor Safety: Acceptable perioperative morbidity in living donors

Recommendation: LDLT should be considered in centers with experience in living donor transplantation. It may be the preferred approach to circumvent deceased donor scarcity.

The recurrence pattern after LT for CRLM is unique and favorable compared to non-transplant patients:

  • Recurrence Rate: 78.3% of patients experience recurrence
  • Median DFS: 11.8 months (disease-free survival)
  • BUT Median OS: 60.3 months overall (excellent despite high recurrence)
  • Median OS After Relapse: 37.1 months (very indolent)
  • 94% of Recurrences: Occur within 2 years of LT

Key Insight: Total hepatectomy removes the primary tumor burden, while residual disease exhibits an indolent pattern with prolonged survival even after relapse. This is the "recurrence paradox" that makes LT effective despite high recurrence rates.

Management of post-LT recurrence requires a multidisciplinary approach:

  • Surveillance: Regular imaging (CT/MRI) and tumor markers (CEA)
  • Site of Recurrence:
    • Pulmonary metastases: Consider resection if feasible
    • Peritoneal disease: Evaluate for cytoreduction + HIPEC
    • Distant metastases: Locoregional or systemic therapy
  • Chemotherapy: Restart or continue systemic therapy
  • Locoregional Therapies: TACE, SIRT, RFA for limited disease
  • Immunosuppression: Careful management to balance rejection risk and anti-tumor immunity

Prognosis: Median OS after relapse is 37.1 months, suggesting that aggressive management of recurrent disease is warranted.

Bridging chemotherapy is essential in the pre-LT management of CRLM:

  • Duration: Minimum 3 months; often 6-12 months during transplant evaluation
  • Goals:
    • Control disease and prevent progression
    • Assess chemotherapy response (prognostic factor)
    • Identify aggressive disease early
    • Optimize disease burden before transplant
  • Regimens: Doublet (FOLFOX, FOLFIRI) or triplet (FOLFOXIRI) ± targeted therapy (anti-EGFR, anti-VEGF)
  • Response Assessment: Progressive disease on chemotherapy is a risk factor (included in Oslo Score)
  • Timing: LT should occur shortly after optimal disease control achieved

Key Point: Adequate response to bridging chemotherapy is a prerequisite for LT consideration.

Yes, several CRLM-specific exclusion criteria have been established by IHPBA:

  • Molecular/Pathological:
    • BRAF V600E mutation (absolute exclusion)
    • Microsatellite instability or mismatch repair deficiency
    • Undifferentiated adenocarcinoma histology
    • Signet ring cell carcinoma
  • Radiological/Clinical:
    • Extrahepatic metastases (except prior resected pulmonary metastases)
    • Local recurrence of primary tumor
    • Inadequate response to bridging chemotherapy
    • Extensive nodal disease (N2 or greater)
  • General Transplant Contraindications:
    • Active infection or uncontrolled sepsis
    • Significant comorbidities precluding surgery
    • Poor performance status (ECOG > 1)
    • Active substance abuse

Recommendation: Use the Patient Selection Decision Tool to systematically assess contraindications.

LT offers superior outcomes compared to alternative approaches for unresectable CRLM:

LT + Chemotherapy 56.6% (ITT)

Key Finding: LT provides a 4.5-fold improvement in survival (HR 0.37) compared to chemotherapy alone in the TransMet RCT.

Immunosuppression in CRLM transplantation requires careful balance:

  • Standard Regimens: Tacrolimus, mycophenolate, and corticosteroids (similar to other LT indications)
  • Unique Consideration: Need to maintain anti-tumor immunity while preventing rejection
  • Everolimus: mTOR inhibitor with potential anti-tumor properties; increasingly used
  • Monitoring: Regular assessment of rejection and recurrence risk
  • Post-LT Chemotherapy: May be continued or restarted depending on recurrence risk

Key Point: Immunosuppression protocols should be individualized based on tumor biology and recurrence risk.

Waitlist time varies significantly by geography and center:

  • Europe: Typically 6-18 months depending on country and MELD score
  • United States: Highly variable; MELD-based allocation means CRLM patients may wait longer
  • Living Donor LT: Can be scheduled more quickly (3-6 months), avoiding waitlist delays
  • Bridging Therapy: Chemotherapy continues during waitlist period to maintain disease control

Recommendation: Early referral is important to optimize bridging therapy timing and account for potential waitlist delays.

Cost and coverage varies significantly:

  • United States: LT for CRLM is increasingly covered by major insurance companies and Medicare
  • Europe: Generally covered by national health systems in countries with established CRLM programs
  • Total Cost: LT typically costs $300,000-$500,000 USD (varies by center and complications)
  • Insurance Verification: Essential to verify coverage before proceeding with evaluation
  • Financial Counseling: Most transplant centers provide financial counseling services

Note: This is a general overview; specific coverage should be confirmed with individual insurance providers.
