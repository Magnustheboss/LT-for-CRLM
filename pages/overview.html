Overview: The Re-emergence of LT for CRLM

The Clinical Problem

Colorectal cancer (CRC) is a highly prevalent malignancy, with approximately 50% of patients developing liver metastases (CRLM) during the course of their disease. While surgical resection offers the only chance of cure, only 20-30% of patients present with technically resectable disease.

For the remaining 70-80% with permanently unresectable, liver-confined CRLM, the prognosis is poor:

  • Palliative chemotherapy: Median OS 24-30 months, 5-year OS <15%
  • Limited treatment options: Disease progression despite modern systemic therapy
  • Need for curative approaches: Patients have no alternative to palliative care

Historical Context: Why LT Was Abandoned

Liver transplantation for CRLM was explored in the 1980s and 1990s but was largely abandoned due to:

  • Poor long-term survival (5-year OS <20%)
  • Limited efficacy of chemotherapy at that time (fluorouracil only, ~20% response rate)
  • Ethical concerns about using scarce donor organs for metastatic disease
  • High rates of early recurrence and mortality unrelated to tumor progression

The Modern Era: Three Key Advances

1. Modern Chemotherapy

Doublet and triplet regimens with targeted therapies (anti-EGFR, anti-VEGF) now achieve 54-80% response rates, enabling better disease control before transplant.

2. Improved Immunosuppression

Tailored regimens using tacrolimus, mycophenolate, and everolimus reduce rejection while maintaining anti-tumor immunity.

3. Better Patient Selection

Understanding of tumor biology and development of prognostic scoring systems (Oslo Score, FCRS, PET-MTV) enable identification of patients with favorable disease.

The Oslo Experience: Paradigm Shift

In 2006, the University of Oslo initiated the SECA-I (Secondary Cancer I) trial, which fundamentally changed the field:

SECA-I Trial (2013)

Parameter Value
Number of Patients 21
5-Year Overall Survival 60%
1-Year Disease-Free Survival 35%
Recurrence Rate 90%
Median Time to Recurrence 6 months

Key Insight: Despite nearly universal recurrence, overall survival was excellent, suggesting that total hepatectomy removes the primary tumor burden while residual disease exhibits an indolent pattern.

SECA-II: Improved Selection Yields Better Outcomes

Building on SECA-I, the SECA-II trial applied stricter selection criteria and achieved even more impressive results:

SECA-II Results

  • 5-Year Overall Survival: Up to 83% in selected cohorts
  • 10-Year Overall Survival: 88.9% (Oslo Score 0-1)
  • 3-Year Disease-Free Survival: 35%
  • 4-Year Survival After Relapse: 73%

This demonstrates that LT outcomes in highly selected CRLM patients can be comparable to those for traditional indications like HCC within Milan criteria.

The TransMet Trial: First RCT Evidence

The TransMet trial (2024) is the first multicenter, randomized controlled trial comparing LT plus chemotherapy to chemotherapy alone:

Current Status: Transplant Oncology Established

LT for CRLM has now transitioned from an experimental concept to a validated clinical practice:

  • International Consensus: IHPBA published comprehensive guidelines in 2021
  • Growing Adoption: Increasing number of centers worldwide performing LT for CRLM
  • Multiple Strategies: Deceased donor LT, living donor LT (LDLT), and innovative surgical techniques (RAPID)
  • Ongoing Trials: 13+ prospective studies investigating optimal patient selection and treatment sequencing
