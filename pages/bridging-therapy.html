Bridging Therapy and Disease Management

Bridging therapy refers to systemic chemotherapy and other treatments administered to patients while awaiting liver transplantation. This page provides comprehensive guidance on chemotherapy regimens, locoregional therapies, and management strategies for patients with unresectable colorectal liver metastases awaiting LT.

Systemic Chemotherapy Regimens

Modern chemotherapy regimens for CRLM are highly effective and form the backbone of bridging therapy. The choice of regimen depends on patient factors, disease characteristics, and treatment goals.

Doublet Regimens

FOLFOX (Fluorouracil, Leucovorin, Oxaliplatin)

Composition: 5-FU + leucovorin + oxaliplatin

Cycle: Every 2 weeks

Response Rate: 40-50%

Advantages:

  • Well-established efficacy and safety
  • Predictable toxicity profile
  • Compatible with targeted therapy
  • Suitable for patients with good performance status

Toxicities:

  • Peripheral neuropathy (cumulative, dose-dependent)
  • Myelosuppression
  • Diarrhea
  • Hand-foot syndrome

Monitoring: CBC, liver function tests, neuropathy assessment every 2 cycles

FOLFIRI (Fluorouracil, Leucovorin, Irinotecan)

Composition: 5-FU + leucovorin + irinotecan

Cycle: Every 2 weeks

Response Rate: 35-45%

Advantages:

  • No cumulative neuropathy (unlike FOLFOX)
  • Effective in KRAS-mutant tumors
  • Good option for patients with oxaliplatin contraindications

Toxicities:

  • Diarrhea (can be severe)
  • Myelosuppression
  • Cholinergic syndrome (early diarrhea)
  • Nausea/vomiting

Monitoring: CBC, liver function tests, diarrhea assessment every 2 cycles

Triplet Regimens

FOLFOXIRI (Fluorouracil, Leucovorin, Oxaliplatin, Irinotecan)

Composition: 5-FU + leucovorin + oxaliplatin + irinotecan

Cycle: Every 2 weeks

Response Rate: 54-65% (highest among standard regimens)

Indications:

  • Extensive unresectable CRLM
  • Patients with good performance status (ECOG 0-1)
  • Fit patients awaiting LT
  • Rapid disease progression

Advantages:

  • Highest response rates
  • Improved progression-free survival
  • Better disease control during waitlist period
  • May improve prognostic scores (Oslo, FCRS, PET-MTV)

Toxicities:

  • Grade 3-4 toxicity in 70-80% of patients
  • Neutropenia
  • Diarrhea
  • Peripheral neuropathy
  • Requires close monitoring and supportive care

Monitoring: CBC, liver function tests, neuropathy assessment every 2 cycles; consider dose modifications for toxicity

Targeted Therapies

Modern chemotherapy regimens are often combined with targeted agents to improve efficacy:

Anti-EGFR Therapy (Cetuximab, Panitumumab)

Indication: KRAS wild-type tumors

Mechanism: Inhibits epidermal growth factor receptor signaling

Efficacy: Improves response rate and OS in KRAS wild-type patients

Toxicities:

  • Acneiform rash (80% of patients)
  • Hypomagnesemia
  • Infusion reactions (cetuximab)

Monitoring: Magnesium levels, skin assessment, infusion reactions

Anti-VEGF Therapy (Bevacizumab, Aflibercept, Ramucirumab)

Indication: All patients (regardless of KRAS status)

Mechanism: Inhibits vascular endothelial growth factor signaling; blocks angiogenesis

Efficacy: Improves response rate and OS when combined with chemotherapy

Toxicities:

  • Hypertension
  • Proteinuria
  • Bleeding risk
  • Thrombosis
  • GI perforation (rare)

Monitoring: Blood pressure, urinalysis, bleeding signs, thrombosis risk

Bridging Therapy Duration and Timing

Minimum Duration

Recommended: Minimum 3 months of systemic chemotherapy before LT consideration

  • Allows assessment of chemotherapy response
  • Identifies patients with aggressive disease
  • Enables disease control before transplant

Optimal Duration

Recommended: 6-12 months of bridging therapy

  • Allows maximum disease burden reduction
  • Provides time for transplant evaluation
  • Optimizes prognostic scores
  • Accounts for waitlist time

Extended Bridging Therapy

Indications: Consider extending beyond 12 months if:

  • Continued response to chemotherapy
  • Improving prognostic scores
  • Decreasing PET-MTV
  • Excellent tolerance to treatment
  • Waitlist time still pending

Timing of LT Relative to Chemotherapy

Optimal Approach: LT should occur shortly after optimal disease control achieved

  • Avoid excessive delays that allow disease progression
  • Complete chemotherapy cycles before transplant if possible
  • Coordinate with transplant center for optimal timing
  • Consider patient factors (toxicity, performance status)

Locoregional Therapies

Locoregional therapies can be used as adjuncts to systemic chemotherapy to enhance disease control during the bridging period.

TACE (Transarterial Chemoembolization)

Mechanism: Delivers chemotherapy directly to tumors via hepatic artery while blocking blood supply

Indications:

  • Adjunct to systemic chemotherapy
  • Patients with stable disease on chemo
  • Bridge to LT in select cases

Efficacy: Improves local control; may improve progression-free survival

Toxicities:

  • Post-embolization syndrome (fever, pain, nausea)
  • Hepatic dysfunction
  • Ischemic complications

SIRT (Selective Internal Radiation Therapy)

Mechanism: Delivers radioactive microspheres to liver tumors via hepatic artery

Indications:

  • Adjunct to systemic chemotherapy
  • Patients with extensive intrahepatic disease
  • Bridge to LT in select cases

Efficacy: Improves local control; may improve time to progression

Advantages:

  • Minimally invasive
  • Well-tolerated
  • Can be repeated if needed

RFA (Radiofrequency Ablation)

Mechanism: Uses heat to destroy tumors percutaneously

Indications:

  • Small lesions (< 3-4 cm)
  • Limited number of tumors
  • Adjunct to systemic therapy

Efficacy: Good local control for small lesions

Limitations:

  • Not suitable for extensive disease
  • Risk of incomplete ablation
  • Limited role in bridging therapy for CRLM

Hepatic Artery Infusion (HAI)

Mechanism: Delivers chemotherapy directly into hepatic artery via implanted pump

Indications:

  • Extensive intrahepatic disease
  • Systemic chemotherapy resistance
  • Bridge to LT in select cases

Efficacy: High response rates; improves time to progression

Considerations:

  • Requires surgical placement of pump
  • Pump-related complications
  • Requires specialized expertise

Response Assessment During Bridging Therapy

Imaging Assessment

Frequency: Every 2-3 months during bridging therapy

Modalities:

  • Contrast-enhanced CT or MRI of liver
  • 18F-FDG PET/CT (baseline and periodic)
  • Chest imaging to exclude pulmonary metastases

Response Criteria

RECIST 1.1 Criteria: Standard assessment for solid tumors

  • Complete Response (CR): Disappearance of all lesions
  • Partial Response (PR): ≥ 30% reduction in sum of longest diameters
  • Stable Disease (SD): < 30% reduction and < 20% increase
  • Progressive Disease (PD): ≥ 20% increase in sum of longest diameters

Prognostic Score Evolution

Reassess Prognostic Scores During Bridging:

  • Oslo Score: Recalculate every 3 months; improved scores indicate favorable response
  • FCRS: May improve with disease burden reduction
  • PET-MTV: Should decrease with effective chemotherapy; < 70 cm³ is favorable

Clinical Significance: Patients with improving prognostic scores during bridging therapy are upgraded from marginal to good candidates for LT.

Management of Recurrent Disease After LT

Despite excellent OS after LT, recurrence occurs in 78.3% of patients. Aggressive management of recurrent disease is warranted given the prolonged survival after relapse (median 37.1 months).

Surveillance Strategy

Recommended Surveillance:

  • Months 0-12: CT/MRI every 2-3 months; CEA every month
  • Months 12-24: CT/MRI every 3 months; CEA every 2 months
  • After 24 months: CT/MRI every 3-4 months; CEA every 3 months
  • PET-CT: Consider annually or if CEA elevation

Sites of Recurrence

Distribution of Recurrence:

  • Pulmonary metastases: 40-50% of recurrences
  • Peritoneal disease: 20-30% of recurrences
  • Distant metastases: 10-20% of recurrences
  • Graft recurrence: 10-15% of recurrences

Treatment of Recurrent Disease

Pulmonary Metastases

Management: Thoracoscopic or open resection if feasible

  • Resection improves OS in selected patients
  • Consider resection if < 4 lesions and good performance status
  • Systemic chemotherapy for unresectable disease

Peritoneal Disease

Management: Consider cytoreductive surgery + HIPEC (hyperthermic intraperitoneal chemotherapy)

  • Selected patients with limited peritoneal involvement
  • Systemic chemotherapy for extensive disease
  • Palliative care for unresectable disease

Systemic Chemotherapy

Management: Restart or continue chemotherapy

  • Resume previous regimen if good response
  • Switch to alternative regimen if prior resistance
  • Consider clinical trials for novel agents
  • Coordinate with transplant team regarding immunosuppression

Locoregional Therapy

Management: TACE, SIRT, or RFA for limited disease

  • Useful for graft recurrence
  • Can be combined with systemic therapy
  • May improve progression-free survival

Immunosuppression and Anti-Tumor Immunity

Balancing immunosuppression to prevent rejection while maintaining anti-tumor immunity is a unique challenge in LT for CRLM.

Standard Immunosuppressive Regimens

  • Calcineurin inhibitor: Tacrolimus (target level 8-12 ng/mL)
  • Antiproliferative agent: Mycophenolate mofetil or azathioprine
  • Corticosteroids: Tapered to low maintenance dose

Everolimus: mTOR Inhibitor with Anti-Tumor Properties

Emerging Role: Everolimus is increasingly used in CRLM transplant patients

  • Mechanism: mTOR inhibition; anti-proliferative effects
  • Potential Anti-Tumor Activity: May reduce recurrence risk
  • Advantages: Allows tacrolimus dose reduction; potential anti-cancer benefit
  • Toxicities: Stomatitis, dyslipidemia, pneumonitis

Post-LT Chemotherapy

Considerations:

  • Chemotherapy can be safely administered post-LT
  • Coordinate with transplant team regarding drug interactions
  • Monitor for increased toxicity due to immunosuppression
  • Assess graft function before initiating chemotherapy
